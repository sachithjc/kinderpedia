What Research Says About Play & Early Learning

A substantial body of research shows that play-based and interactive learning supports early brain development, strengthens cognitive skills, and makes learning meaningful and engaging for young children. Key findings from peer-reviewed research include:

  • Play supports healthy brain development: Neuroscience and pediatric research show that play contributes to the formation of neural connections, enhances memory and attention, and supports executive functions essential for learning (AAP Pediatrics).
  • Play boosts cognitive and problem-solving skills: Research indicates that play-based learning encourages exploration, experimentation, and active learning, leading to stronger cognitive development (Siviy et al.).
  • Interactive formats enhance engagement: Studies show that interactive, game-like learning experiences improve engagement and learning outcomes in young children (Nijhof et al.).
  • Play supports social and emotional development: Play-based learning helps children develop emotional regulation, collaboration, and social skills ‚Äî key foundations for school readiness (Early Childhood Research).
  • Play reduces stress and supports well-being: Evidence suggests that playful learning environments reduce anxiety and help children feel safe, confident, and motivated to learn (Play & Development Studies).
